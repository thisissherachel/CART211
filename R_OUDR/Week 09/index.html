~ Little blog for my readings ~

Please see below summaries and comments on the readings assigned for CART211

WEEK 01

When watching this video, I was quite surprised by the different type of advances that were brought out to the public at the same time. The mouse and keyboard seem reasonable for the late 1960s but when I saw the collaborative working space and the video conferencing, I was quite surprised. I only started collaborative work, or at last found out it existed through google pages, about 10 years ago. It was fun to see Engelbart explain all the concepts to a public that must be so excited by these new developments. It allowed me to live through them a little and share that excitement.


WEEK 02

Seeing and the evolution of the internet's use going from military to academia to public along with the evolution of the network centralization, decentralization, and privatization was very interesting. Especially when we see today what it’s become. But thinking about something mentioned, which was that the internet was the greatest invention since electricity, it got me thinking about something I also saw which how impactful glass was in history. It raises the question of the limits of internet without glass (i.e a computer screen). That said, one could say how the internet raised the importance of a screen and pushed its development.


WEEK 03

It was beautiful to see the article dissect the complex shift between the computer as a bureaucratic counting machine to a communal intelligence machine. Talking about the contributions of Stuart Brand with his counterculture bible the Whole Earth Catalog, it was very interesting to see how this communal information concept was able to inspire so many engineers in different ways and for different reasons but still have the same conclusion: computers have communal intelligence potential. That said, it is funny to read this article preaching about machine and their ability for communal intelligence and hence collective growth but then see today how everything evolved. The communal access to information today seems to drive away from the idea of collective growth and more into the idea of individualism and the increase in extremism. Rather using the information to grow as a collective, it is used to differentiate one another.


WEEK 04

Reading the history of how art implicated itself in the newly immergence of the internet seemed inevitable however, the discussion on how the internet impacted the art community was really something to appreciate. Greene speaks about how the internet began to empower artists through its ability to facilitate communication and community which in return allowed much more visibility to artists. The exposure, inspiration, and breaking of boundaries was like never seem before. It’s never something I thought about but the immergence of Net.art was absolutely revolutionary for artists. It’s easy to take advantage of the amount of access and how large of a following or platform one can have with the use of the internet today. This aspect of the internet really is a beautiful benefit of its creation.


WEEK 05

In this piece there was a lot of great ‘one-liners’. Here I’ll share a few that struck a cord in me and hopefully you can also take a little something from them even if they’re taken out of context: “everything is anything else”, “in a cultural climate where we have accepted that the singular qualification for the moniker “art” is the intention of any one individual to label it as such.”, “insist on a participatory view of a culture at large, and ultimately of taking iconoclasm itself as a quotidian activity”, “Ironically, the most radical and “progressive” movements of the Post-Internet period would be those who either pass by either largely unnoticed due to a decision to opt out of any easily-accessible distribution networks, or else would be composed of a community of people producing cultural objects not intended as artistic propositions and not applying themselves with the label of artist.”, “extraordinary is now also the ordinary—the myth is also the everyday”, “Dealing with language can too forcibly illustrate the thoughts behind an image, or belittle a work if the text is not as clever or aesthetic as the image itself”, and lastly “move seamlessly from physical representation to Internet representation, either changing for each context, built with an intention of universality, or created with a deliberate irreverence for either venue of transmission”. I pulled all these because as an artist myself (very not post-internet of me to say), these made me really question my practice in today’s era, culture, and my placement in society in regards to everything.


WEEK 06

This article brought up a lot of issues with crypto that I was unaware of but also dived into really the pros and cons of it all. It talked about the environmental impact of NFTs, how NFTs opened doors for many digital artists, the recent art movement of crypto art that is evolving from it all, the political divergence of crypto’s original socialist intent, the inequality that still is very present even in the digital and physical art world, and generally what blockchains are, why they are being used, different ways they could be used, and the reality of them today post pandemic. All of this is definitely overwhelming. A part of me wants to participate as an artist, a part of me wants to boycott or find better eco-conscious ways, and another part of me just wants us to get to green energy already so we can all chill a little. This article did not help with my capitalist, patriarchal, and climate crisis dread.


WEEK 07

Reading Lessig’s “Free Culture” really just reminds me how much of a capitalist society we all live in. This question of ownership is very much linked to the way our society is built, or at least how it evolved and that is in the direction of late-stage capitalism. Growing up in this society, it’s hard to image one without ownership. Ownership is in almost all aspects of our lives, even relationships. However, this idea of normalizing the giving of credit but allowing the right to build upon seems very much like my socialist society dream.


Similar to the last text, this is again just a fight against late-stage capitalism. A fight against this idea of ownership because we all know this concept was just created to maximize profit for the 1%. There’s always been this false claim against socialist ideals that without the capitalist monetary push there would be no source of motivation. This is false and there are multiple sources to prove it. Especially with such an interactive field as programming. Positive reinforcement, contributing to society, personal interest, and a passion for the field is enough. Programming is a field that is already very much based in the sharing of resources and sharing of work through this idea of “programming is just a bunch “copy-pasting”” so I believe that it should remain that way and if not, be reinforced through all software.


WEEK 08

There were two things that stood out to me in this piece: the confirmation that yet another field was impacted by a patriarchal and socially insensitive system and the idea of software and transparency. These two things were those I’ve read about or pondered a little before but seeing it in a different light is always nice. I read another piece recently titled “The Intersection between Culture, Gender, and Technology” by Patrick D. Hopkins which wasn’t as intense as this one where the term “slave” was thrown around for women computing but it exposed the evolution of such a state related to tech. How software or hardware built in this state can affect or hinder future generations. Subsequently, the idea of software and transparency…I’ve always just had a deep sense that software was the epidemy of transparency. Nothing can be created nor destroyed but especially in tech because all your interactions with the computer are recorded to be computed…That said, it’s still unclear to me where deleted folders go…


WEEK 09

There was no reading this week :)
